§ 02 · The Past
004 / 017
— Chapter Two / Past

For two decades, every checkout assumed
a pair of human eyes.

1995 → 2023

The assumption that held — then didn't.

From the Amazon 1-Click patent (1999) to Stripe's payments.js (2011), the entire web stack was tuned for one shape of commerce: a human, in a browser, dragging a finger across a screen.

CAPTCHAs, 3-D Secure pop-ups, device fingerprints, behavior biometrics, cart abandonment rescue — all of it was engineered around the pixel-level rhythm of a person hesitating on a page.

Then, between Feb 2024 and Apr 2025, four things happened in quick succession. The rhythm changed.

Four shocks, one inflection.

  • 2024 · 02 Amazon Rufus launches in beta — the first platform-scale shopping agent.
  • 2024 · 07 Rufus opens to all US users; conversations displace queries.
  • 2025 · 04 Four industry moves in a single month: A2A, Buy for Me, Visa Intelligent Commerce, Mastercard Agent Pay.
  • 2025 · 05 Coinbase revives HTTP 402 — a 30-year-old status code — for native on-chain payments.
  • 2025 · 08 Amazon begins blocking major AI crawlers — a new protectionism emerges.
  • 2025 · 09 ChatGPT Instant Checkout ships — the first billion-user agent commerce product.

The web that grew up around a human customer is now being rebuilt for a different one entirely.

§ 03 · Present · The outlier
008 / 017
— The walled garden

Amazon Buy for Me — or, the case against going it alone.

live in the US · 500k+ SKU

A bet on the opposite direction.

While Google publishes open protocols and Stripe open-sources SDKs, Amazon went the other way: an end-to-end, in-house agent that browses third-party brand sites and checks out on behalf of the user — inside the Amazon app.

The business case is real — Rufus produced US$12 billion in incremental sales in 2025. But the strategy is already under pressure.

Forbes: "Google builds open protocol rails. Amazon builds a walled garden."

  • 28%chargeback rate on agent-delegated purchases — triple the web average.
  • DOUBLEAmazon blocks external AI crawlers via robots.txt — while its own agent scrapes third-party sites.
  • IPBobo Design Studio and others escalate to IP lawyers over unauthorised scraping.
  • OPT-OUTBrands are enrolled by default; explicit opt-out required.
  • DMAEU Digital Markets Act likely to force open-agent access on gatekeepers.

The lesson for every large retailer: openness is not a virtue here — it is a moat. Brands without a standards strategy will be shopped on someone else's terms.

§ 03 · Three philosophies
010 / 017
— The three bets

Open protocol, card-network extension, or walled garden.

Philosophy matters more than features
BET 01 · OPEN

The HTTP bet.

If agent commerce becomes critical infrastructure, open standards always win the long game — HTTP over AOL, TCP/IP over OSI, HTML over Flash.

  • UCP for discovery and orchestration.
  • AP2 Mandates for cryptographic authorization.
  • ACP for checkout, x402 for micropayments.
Google · OpenAI · Stripe · Coinbase
BET 02 · CARD NETWORKS

The incumbents' move.

Fiat settlement will still flow through card rails. The real fight is to hold on to the trust layer — the part that justifies 1.5 – 3% interchange.

  • Visa bets "standard" — TAP signatures verified at the CDN.
  • Mastercard bets "platform" — agent registry + Agentic Token.
  • 275M+ combined merchant endpoints, near-zero migration cost.
Visa · Mastercard · FIDO Alliance
BET 03 · WALLED GARDEN

The AOL bet.

Keep the user inside. Build your own agent. Block the others at robots.txt. Protect US$56B of retail-media revenue.

  • Buy for Me · 500k+ SKUs, but 28% chargeback rate.
  • Antitrust exposure — EU DMA, US FTC.
  • Long run: most likely demoted to "super-merchant" on open rails.
Amazon · potentially Apple, Meta

§ 05 · Future · Convergence
012 / 017
— Prediction

By 2030, the seven protocols will have collapsed into three layers.

our expected end-state

Three wins. Not one.

The historical pattern is clear: infrastructure markets do not produce a single winner — they produce a stack. Open protocols take orchestration. Card networks hold trust and fiat settlement. Crypto-native rails own micropayments and agent-to-agent transfers.

Walled gardens, if they survive, do so as super-merchants on open rails — the way Amazon today accepts Visa.

"Visa is betting on the standard. Mastercard is betting on the platform. Amazon is betting on the experience. Google, OpenAI and Coinbase are betting on the protocol. All four can be right — at different layers."
